2-Bit Calculators and Class Discussion/Readings

Full implementation of a 2-Bit Addtion calculator, and a 2-Bit Subtraction calculator.

Creating and developing a 2-bit addition calculator meant that I had to account for a carry-out bit when adding single-bit number values. This was done with a half-adder that had an output for the carry-out value. This also meant that when the second addition occurred for the second-bit values, I had to account for the possible carry-in value that could exist if there was a carry-out in the single-bit addition. The figure above depicts the full adder that takes into account the possibility of a carry-in input. The figure to the right showcases the utilization of both logical circuits to create the circuit block that makes up the 2-bit calculator. This full implementation works for all 2-bit value additions and accounts for any possible carry-in and carry-out bits. Below the 2-bit addition calculator is a simple trace-through example for inputs X = 11 (3) and Y = 01 (note that subscripts of the variables determine their bit position).

2-Bit Subtractor calculator


Half-Adder: Inputs: X_0 = 1, Y_0 = 0 Outputs: Borrow_Out_Half = 0, Difference_0 = 1
Full-Adder: Inputs: X_1 = 1, Y_1 = 1, Borrow_In = 0 Outputs: Difference_1 = 0, Borrow_Out = 0
Answer: 001

Creating and developing a 2-bit subtraction calculator meant that I had to account for a borrow-out bit when subtracting single-bit number values. This also meant that for the second value bits, I had to account for a borrow-in from the single-bit subtraction. Hence, there are three inputs for the full subtractor depicted in the image above. However, subtraction was much more tedious since the logic had to account for which value was being subtracted from which. In my implementation, the subtraction always occurs in the format X − Y, which means that whatever value is used for X is subtracted by Y. Furthermore, the truth table (not pictured here) is much more confusing in terms of the logical operations needed to accurately achieve the outputs for the differences and borrow-out. The figure to the left showcases the utilization of both logical circuits to create the circuit block that makes up the 2-bit calculator. This full implementation works for all 2-bit value subtractions and accounts for any possible borrow-in and borrow-out bits. It is important to note that this calculator does not represent negative values, only the differences between values and borrow-outs. Reflection On In Class Discussions and Readings

Reflection On Discussion Questions

Before the in-depth discussions we had in class, I would have only labeled laptops, desktops, and phones as computers. Yes, I would have made caveats to certain things depending on their components, but I had a much more narrow criterion for what constitutes a computer. However, the in-class questions like, “Is a broken computer still a computer?” and “Is a discharged or turned-off computer still a computer?” made me think much more about what makes a computer a computer. I recall making the analogy of a broken car to a broken computer. Specifically, I made the point that if something is broken, it does not take away the original use it served (such as being a car that could be driven around). There was a slippery slope I encountered where we could not determine the threshold for what could be broken and still be considered a broken computer. I do recall that Joe brought up the concept of a pile of sand, where we reach a conceptual breakdown of how much you can remove until it no longer is a “pile” of sand. We can make endless criteria for those situations, and they break down what I argued for in the question.

Considering the same question, I recall Hudson's argument, which focused on simply adding descriptors to objects. The central claim in his response was that if, at one point, an object served a certain purpose, it could still retain that object's name. Fundamentally, he argued that if a computer worked as a computer at some point, then it would still be labeled a computer with an additional descriptor to portray its current state. Therefore, if a computer worked at some point but is now broken, then we simply call it a broken computer to describe it. However, this opened the door to how we generate criteria for objects based on their utility to us. This led to the question, “Does a computer need to be used by, or have the capacity to be used by, humans?” in our next discussion. We reached a general consensus in class that the utility of a computer is essential to its definition. Humans coined the terminology and the foundation of what we consider computers today; therefore, it must be that they have some utility for us. If they are unusable, then they serve no purpose to us and would not be categorized as a computer within our conception and description of a computer. This made me think back on the possibilities of non-physical objects being considered computers, considering they could still be utilized by us.

Reflection On Readings

I have always imagined computers to be inanimate in nature, and this includes all of their components as well. Hence, the research paper titled “Integrating Living Organisms in Devices to Implement Care-based Interactions” by Jasmine Lu and Pedro Lopes (Lu & Lopes, 2022) was a little concerning in its implications. I do recall what a Tamagotchi was, and I had similar games growing up that relied on nurturing digital creatures to achieve some goal or generate affection from me to further interact with the creature or app. However, at the end of the day, I knew the creature was not real and eventually became bored or moved on from the game itself. There was no harm to the creature since it was digital and inanimate, but this paper introduces the idea of introducing a living organism into computers to ensure prolonged interaction between the user and device. The experiment utilized a slime mold in a heart rate sensor, anchoring the user to nurture the mold so that the device could perform its function. While I do not believe there is any ill intent in the nature of the experiment, I do believe that it could have detrimental implications if we are to assume that living organisms can be used as components for computers. The slime mold has adapted to poor conditions and thus is very forgiving if users forget or simply ignore taking care of it. This is not true for all living organisms, so I doubt there are any larger applications where this could ensure user interaction. Furthermore, using other living organisms seems morally wrong if they possess autonomy that can be constrained or stripped. While I do think the paper provided an interesting application of employing human empathy to generate user interaction with devices, I do not think the larger implications or use of it are entirely good in nature.


My previous work with logical operators in both Math of CS and Math Structures made me recall much about the complexity and usage of logical operators for propositions. This class made me question the idea of placing these operators and propositions in the same category as a computer. I would have previously denied this claim, but articles such as “Robust Soldier Crab Ball Gate” (Gunji, Nishiyama, & Adamatzky, 2011) swayed me into believing that these logical operands and propositions are the basis of computers. There are more nuances to this idea, and a catalyst has to be present so that the operations can be performed (in computers, this is electricity). The use of crabs as a source for this catalyst made me analyze my preconception of what a computer was. At its core, computers have logic gates that can perform propositions to reach answers. This is where my previous conception of the physicality of computers was altered, and I realized that the calculators we made are also essentially computers, albeit very simple and concise in their purpose.
